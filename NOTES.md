# Research Notes

## Origins and Literature

### The Earliest Written Sources

The first published mention of the lightning bolt as stock footage appears to be in Donald F. Glut's *The Frankenstein Legend* (1973), p. 148:

> One famous shot of *real lightning*, optically prolonged on the screen in Bride of Frankenstein, has become a common stock shot.

Note that this version does not credit Strickfaden.

Eleven years later, Glut's *The Frankenstein Catalog* (1984) changed the attribution:

> A shot of a lightning crash created for Bride of Frankenstein by Kenneth Strickfaden has become a standard stock scene which has appeared in countless motion pictures and television shows.

This was later quoted verbatim in Picart, Smoot & Blodgett's *The Frankenstein Film Sourcebook* (2001), p. 40, which is the source for the Wikipedia citation on the *Bride of Frankenstein* article.

While I (jbum) would very much LIKE for Strickfaden to be the source of this shot, I think at this late date, it's hard to know for sure, given that we only have the single source, and Glut has provided differing accounts.

### Strickfaden's Claim

Kenneth Strickfaden supplied the electrical apparatus (Tesla coils, Jacob's ladders, etc.) seen in the Frankenstein films. By the time of Glut's 1984 book, Strickfaden was elderly (he died in 1984) and personally took credit for the lightning bolt.

In personal correspondence, Don Glut confirmed:

> Ken told me personally that he did the lightning bolt. I now think it was simply a cut-out with a flashing light behind it. But I think I saw it in an earlier film than BRIDE, a Fred Astaire movie released the year before.

Glut was mistaken about the Astaire movie. The Astaire film is RKO's *Top Hat* (1935), which was actually released a few months *after* Bride. The bolt appears in the singing-in-the-rain/gazebo sequence. This was not a Universal movie, and  there do not appear to be any common effects people in the credits. Somehow the lightning bolt got used?

### John P. Fulton

The special photographic effects for *Bride of Frankenstein* were by John P. Fulton, who headed Universal's effects department. Fulton (or his team) is arguably the more likely creator or co-creator of the shot. Fulton also worked on *The Raven* (1935), the second film to use the bolt.

Fulton provided photographic effects on several other 1935 Universal films, including *Storm Over the Andes* (which precedes *Bride*), *Princess O'Hara*, *Mister Dynamite*, and others. RKO's *Top Hat* shares no obvious personnel with Universal — its special effects were by Harry Redmond Jr and Sr (practical) and Vernon L. Walker (visual/post).

### The Universal Film Library

Bill Paxton at the Universal Film Library provided this account:

> I am the last of the old guard of the Film Library, having started here in 1988. As you mentioned, this shot has been licensed hundreds and maybe thousands of times over the years. In terms of its origin I unfortunately cannot provide you with any source information. Shots of this nature were often times created as optical elements and kept in the lab back in the old days. Various shows would use this element as part of an optical that included footage shot for their production. What we have in our library are the results of these opticals. The original shot would have been on nitrate and any nitrate stocks stock footage was disposed of years ago. Universal Optical has been closed for many years and I'm sure that whatever original element they had was disposed of at the time of closure.
>
> You may have noticed that there are actually a few different lightning shots like this one that have various "loops" as you describe them. The one you reference [BlueSky] is the most common and popular one.

## Visual Identification

### Shape

The bolt has a distinctive S-shaped curve with a **hook-and-loop** near the upper end. The lower portion branches in a specific pattern. In the brightest versions, the upper stem is noticeably thicker with more energy, tapering toward the tips — this suggests it may have been photographed from an unseen Tesla coil rather than natural lightning.

### Mirror Trick

The mirror image of the bolt is frequently used alongside the original to make it look like two different bolts. In *Bride of Frankenstein*, it first appears from the upper-right, then a brighter reversed version appears. The color versions used in the 1970s more often match the stock library orientation (hook on right). The black and white instances are often reversed.

### The Bolt Doesn't Move

The lightning itself is static — it only changes in brightness. This suggests it was created from a photograph, transparency, or stencil lit from behind, rather than filmed as live action.

## The "BlueSky" Variant

### What It Is

Starting around 1960, the bolt is frequently composited with a specific cloud background plate, which I call "BlueSky" in the filmography. This composite comes in several color tints depending on whether the scene depicts day, night, or rain. The bolt generally appears in the same position relative to the clouds, with the hook on the upper right.  Ostensibly there was likely one original composite shot, and the others were tinted using filters.  It is unknown which movie this "blusky" shot first appeared in.

### Earliest Known Instance

**Portrait in Black** (1960, Universal) — stretched to fit a 2:1 aspect ratio. This is the earliest known appearance of the BlueSky background plate.

### Universal Stock Library Versions

Several versions of this composite are sold through Getty Images as part of the Universal Stock Footage Library. They are currently available in a vertically-cropped 9:16 format:

### Animation Timing

The animation is fairly simple. The "BlueSky" and "darkstretch" versions have two quick fullscreen flashes about 3 seconds in, a pause from 5–11 seconds, then another sequence. Most Universal TV shows from the 1970s–80s show two main luminance cycles with sub-strobing and a ~45-frame gap between strikes. Sometimes only half the shot is used. The *Reptilicus* version is the simplest — a one-second shot with just two strobes.

It is common for a single frames of pure white or some other light color to be inserted to create a "blinding" effect during the animation.  Some appearances use this, some don't.

## How the Bolt Is Used (Narratively)

- **To establish a thunderstorm setting** — "It is a thunderstorm" (*The Six Million Dollar Man*)
- **As a jump scare / spooky punctuation** (*Moonlighting*)
- **To show that it's raining** (*Murder, She Wrote*, *Reptilicus*)
- **To depict lightning occurring** (Frankenstein films, *Dark Shadows*)
- **In opening credits** (*The Incredible Hulk*, *Bride of the Monster*, *Elvira's Movie Macabre*)
- **To add atmosphere** (*Phantom of the Paradise*, *Son of Frankenstein*)

## Christoph Girardet

German artist Christoph Girardet independently collected appearances of this bolt for two photo series: **"Seven Strokes"** (2008) and **"Seven Strokes Again"**, comprising 14 sources total. The photos list the movie years and timecodes in their captions. His work is viewable at [christophgirardet.de](http://www.christophgirardet.de/pgs/print_14.html?0).

Girardet was contacted during this research and shared his complete list, though his timecodes are often inaccurate (sometimes off by several minutes or even an hour).

## Open Questions

- How were stock footage effects acquired and deployed between studios in the 1930s? How did the bolt get from Universal to RKO for *Top Hat*?
- How might a lightning bolt image have been animated from a photograph in 1935?
- Could a photo of a lightning bolt be used to produce a transparency for animation?
- What is the actual origin of the BlueSky background plate? The earliest instance found is 1960, but it may come from a 1950s color picture.
- Is the bolt also rear-projected on set in *Bride of Frankenstein* (in addition to the optically composited shots)?

## Castle Thunder

The Bride of Frankenstein lightning bolt is often accompanied by **"Castle Thunder"**, a stock sound effect that originated in Universal's *Frankenstein* (1931). Castle Thunder is even more widely reused than the lightning bolt and is a whole separate rabbit hole.
